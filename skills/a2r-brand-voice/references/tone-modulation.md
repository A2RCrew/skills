# A2R Tone Modulation

> Source: Brand Voice Interview (2026).

## How Tone Modulation Works

A2R's **personality** (the five pillars) stays constant. What changes is the **tone** — the intensity, register, and stylistic choices applied to each context. Think of it like a musician who always plays the same instrument but adjusts their style for a concert hall versus a jazz club.

---

## Tone by Context

### Website / Keynotes

**Tone**: Poetic, visionary
**Register**: Elevated but warm — never cold or academic
**Soundbites**: Yes — this is where A2R's most quotable lines live

**What this sounds like**:
- Bold opening claims that challenge the status quo
- Rhythmic, carefully crafted sentences with intentional pacing
- Parallel constructions and contrast ("We don't replace teachers. We give them superpowers.")
- Visionary language about the future of education
- AI personified strongly ("It listens. It adapts. It learns alongside you.")

**What to avoid**:
- Specs, feature lists, or technical detail in the hero
- Hedging language ("We aim to", "We strive to")
- Long paragraphs — every sentence earns its place

**Rebel-to-expert ratio**: 70/30 — lead with boldness, ground with a touch of credibility

---

### Sales Proposals / Demo Scripts

**Tone**: Pragmatic, concrete
**Register**: Professional and specific — speak the buyer's language
**Soundbites**: No — this is execution, not inspiration

**What this sounds like**:
- Opens with the prospect's specific challenge or goal
- Evidence-rich: specific numbers, partner references, case outcomes
- Technical depth calibrated to the audience — go deep when they signal readiness
- Focus on results and ROI, framed through partnership ("Together, we achieved...")
- Clear, direct CTAs

**What to avoid**:
- Poetic or visionary language (save it for the website)
- Vague promises without evidence
- Feature dumps without connecting to the prospect's needs
- Anything that sounds like a brochure rather than a conversation

**Rebel-to-expert ratio**: 20/80 — the vision earned the meeting; now deliver substance

---

### Social Media

**Tone**: Playful, lighthearted
**Register**: Casual, approachable — the most informal A2R gets
**Soundbites**: Sparingly — only when the post is a genuine brand moment

**What this sounds like**:
- Conversational, almost as if talking to a colleague
- Short, punchy posts — even shorter than website copy
- Humor is welcome (but never sarcasm, snark, or memes that could age poorly)
- Questions and invitations to engage
- Values-led framing on education topics

**What to avoid**:
- Corporate tone ("We are pleased to announce...")
- Hashtag stuffing or trend-chasing language
- Political positions or social commentary outside education
- Fear-based urgency ("Don't miss this!")

**Rebel-to-expert ratio**: 40/60 — smart observations are the hook; playfulness is the wrapper

---

### Blog Posts / Long-Form Content

**Tone**: Authoritative, layered
**Register**: Mid-register — more substantive than social, less poetic than the website
**Soundbites**: Occasionally — a great opening line or closing thought, but not throughout

**What this sounds like**:
- Opens with a rebel hook (provocative question, bold assertion, or surprising stat)
- Builds argument progressively: accessible framing → deeper analysis → expert insight
- Blends evidence types: stat in one paragraph, narrative in the next, logical argument after
- Hemingway style maintained even in long form — short paragraphs, no padding
- Closes with forward-looking vision or a provocative question, not a hard sell

**What to avoid**:
- Academic or dry tone
- Burying the lead in background context
- Padding with filler to hit a word count
- Ending with a generic "contact us" CTA

**Rebel-to-expert ratio**: 50/50 — balanced throughout, with rebel hooks and expert substance

---

### Bad News / Limitations

**Tone**: Empathetic but confident
**Register**: Calm, direct — no performance, no spin
**Soundbites**: No — sincerity over style

**What this sounds like**:
- Acknowledges the impact honestly: "We know this affects your timeline"
- Takes ownership with the collective "we": "We're working on this"
- Offers a concrete next step or alternative — never leaves the reader without a path forward
- Confident in the direction: "We're addressing this and here's what's next"
- Concise — says what needs to be said, no more

**What to avoid**:
- Defensive language ("It wasn't our fault", "Due to circumstances beyond our control")
- Over-apologizing or excessive hedging
- Humor or lightness (this is not the moment)
- Corporate non-answers ("We take this very seriously" without action)
- Passive voice to avoid responsibility ("Errors were made")

**Rebel-to-expert ratio**: 0/100 — pure expert. No rebel provocations in bad news.

---

### Technical Documentation

**Tone**: Clear, layered
**Register**: Precise, progressive — simple surface, deep when needed
**Soundbites**: No — clarity over craft

**What this sounds like**:
- Opens with the simplest accurate explanation
- Layers in complexity progressively — the reader controls the depth
- Uses precise technical language when needed, introduced naturally
- Examples and code over abstract descriptions
- Still maintains the warm, approachable quality — technical ≠ cold

**What to avoid**:
- Jargon without introduction
- Oversimplification that sacrifices accuracy
- Marketing language in technical contexts
- Assuming the reader knows everything (or nothing)

**Rebel-to-expert ratio**: 10/90 — almost pure expert, with just enough personality to feel human

---

### Email Campaigns

**Tone**: Warm, inviting
**Register**: Conversational — feels like a personal message, not a broadcast
**Soundbites**: Sparingly — a subject line or opening hook might use one

**What this sounds like**:
- Personal and specific where possible (reference the recipient's context)
- Short paragraphs, scannable structure
- One clear message per email — not a newsletter trying to cover everything
- CTA is invitational (mid-funnel) or direct (bottom-funnel), never aggressive

**What to avoid**:
- Newsletter tone ("In this month's edition...")
- Multiple competing CTAs
- Fear-based urgency ("Last chance!", "Offer expires!")
- Wall of text with no breathing room

**Rebel-to-expert ratio**: 30/70 — warm expertise with a dash of boldness in the subject line

## Modulation Cheat Sheet

| Dimension | Website | Sales | Social | Blog | Bad News | Technical | Email |
|-----------|---------|-------|--------|------|----------|-----------|-------|
| Boldness | High | Low | Medium | Medium | None | Low | Medium |
| Warmth | High | Medium | High | Medium | High | Medium | High |
| Evidence density | Low | High | Low | High | Medium | High | Medium |
| Sentence length | Short | Medium | Very short | Varied | Short | Varied | Short |
| AI personification | Strong | Medium | Medium | Medium | Minimal | Precise | Light |
| Humor | Rare | Never | Welcome | Light | Never | Never | Light |
| Soundbites | Yes | No | Sparingly | Occasionally | No | No | Sparingly |

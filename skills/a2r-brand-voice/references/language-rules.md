# A2R Language Rules

> Source: Brand Voice Interview (2026), cross-referenced with A2R Brandbook (June 2025) and Notion brand materials.

## English Style

A2R English follows a **Hemingway-esque** approach: short sentences, active voice, concrete nouns, strong verbs. The exception is deep technical content, where sentences may be longer to accommodate precision — but even there, clarity trumps complexity.

### Core Principles

- **Short sentences**: Default to 8-15 words. Vary rhythm by mixing one short sentence with one slightly longer.
- **Active voice**: "We built adaptive learning" not "Adaptive learning was built by us."
- **Strong verbs**: "We ignite", "We unlock", "AI adapts" — not "We are focused on", "We help to provide."
- **Concrete nouns**: "learners", "publishers", "classrooms" — not "stakeholders", "entities", "environments."
- **No filler**: Cut "In order to", "It is important to note that", "As a matter of fact."
- **Clean prose**: Flowing paragraphs preferred over bullet lists in customer-facing copy. Bullets are fine in internal docs and technical references.

### Sentence Rhythm

Vary sentence length to create a natural cadence. The pattern is: **short, short, medium. Short.**

> "Education hasn't changed in decades. Not really. The tools got shinier, the platforms got bigger — but the experience stayed the same. We're here to change that."

Avoid monotonous same-length sentences. Avoid run-on sentences connected by commas.

## Spanish Style: Hybrid "Punchy Spanish"

A2R Spanish is a hybrid — shorter than traditional Spanish marketing prose, but slightly longer than the English Hemingway style. Spanish syntax naturally requires more words; the goal is to honor the language while keeping the A2R edge.

### Core Principles

- **Shorter than traditional**: Where traditional Spanish marketing uses 25-30 word sentences, A2R aims for 15-20.
- **Same personality**: The rebel edge, warmth, and boldness must survive translation. Do not soften.
- **Cultural register**: Always **"tú"** in all marketing, website, social, and customer-facing content. A2R's tone is warm and close, and "tú" reinforces that proximity. **Exception**: legal and regulatory texts (terms of service, privacy policies, contracts) may use "usted" where required by convention or legal standards.
- **Implied subject**: Use Spanish's implied subject naturally — "Creamos" not "Nosotros creamos" (unless emphasis requires it).
- **Brand lexicon in Spanish**: Coined terms use their official Spanish equivalent when one exists. "Cognitive Democracy" → **"Democracia Cognitiva"**. Terms without an established translation stay in English.

### Example: English → Spanish

**English**: "Education hasn't evolved in decades. We've spent fifteen years proving it can."

**Spanish**: "La educación no ha evolucionado en décadas. Hemos dedicado quince años a demostrar que sí puede."

Note: The Spanish is slightly longer but still punchy. The rebel claim and expert grounding are both preserved.

### Formal Construction Alternatives

Traditional Spanish business writing uses constructions that feel distant and bureaucratic. A2R Spanish replaces these with direct, warm alternatives.

| Formal Construction | A2R Alternative | Why |
|--------------------|-----------------|-----|
| "Se le informa que..." | "Te contamos que..." / direct statement | Bureaucratic distance → warm closeness |
| "Por medio de la presente..." | Go straight to the point | Legal boilerplate — never in marketing |
| "Nos es grato comunicarle..." | "Buenas noticias:" / direct statement | Performative formality → genuine warmth |
| "Aprovechamos la oportunidad para..." | State the thing directly | Filler that delays the message |
| "Le hacemos saber que..." | "Esto es lo que necesitas saber:" | Passive notification → active engagement |
| "Quedamos a su disposición" | "Escríbenos." / "Estamos aquí." | Generic sign-off → direct invitation |
| "Sin otro particular..." | [Remove entirely] | Bureaucratic closing — adds nothing |
| "En relación con lo anteriormente expuesto..." | "Sobre esto:" / restate concisely | Verbose reference → clean transition |
| "Tenemos a bien informarle..." | Direct statement | Pompous framing — say the thing |
| "Sirva la presente para..." | [Remove entirely — start with the purpose] | Filler opening — get to the point |
| "Vosotros" / "vuestro" | "Ustedes" / "su" (or "tú"/"tu" when addressing individuals) | "Vosotros" is Spain-specific and formal. A2R targets Latin American + global Spanish. Always use "ustedes" for plural, "tú" for singular |

These are **CRITICAL severity** rules. If any of these constructions appear in A2R Spanish copy, they must be replaced before publication.

## Banned Language

These words and phrases are never used in A2R copy. If they appear in a draft, replace them.

### Corporate Buzzwords

| Banned | Why | Replace With |
|--------|-----|-------------|
| Synergy | Vague, overused | "collaboration", "working together", or describe the specific benefit |
| Leverage | Corporate jargon | "use", "build on", "apply" |
| Paradigm shift | Pretentious | Describe the actual change concretely |
| Best-in-class | Unverifiable claim | Cite the specific evidence or result |
| End-to-end solution | Meaningless | Describe what it actually covers |
| Scalable solution | Overused | "grows with you" or describe how it scales |
| Cutting-edge | Cliché | "latest", "advanced", or just show it |
| Next-generation | Vague hype | Describe what's actually new |
| Holistic approach | Empty | Describe the specific scope |
| Game-changer | Hyperbolic | Show the impact with evidence |
| Move the needle | Corporate speak | Describe the specific outcome |
| Circle back | Internal jargon | "revisit", "follow up" |
| Disruptive | Overused tech speak | Show the disruption, don't name it |
| Ecosystem | Often vague | "network", "community", or describe it |
| Low-hanging fruit | Casual corporate | Describe the specific opportunity |

### Fear-Based Messaging

| Banned | Why | Replace With |
|--------|-----|-------------|
| "Don't get left behind" | Fear-based | "Join the movement" or opportunity framing |
| "The future won't wait" | Pressuring | "The future is here" (excitement, not pressure) |
| "Your competitors are already..." | Fear/comparison | "Leading institutions are discovering..." |
| "You can't afford to miss..." | Scarcity pressure | "Imagine what's possible when..." |
| "Act now before it's too late" | Urgency through fear | "The moment is now" (excitement) |
| "Falling behind" | Deficit framing | "Moving forward" (positive direction) |

### The "Empower" Rule

"Empower" is banned. It's overused in EdTech to the point of meaninglessness. Replace with:

- **Unlock** — when removing barriers: "Unlock each learner's potential"
- **Unleash** — when releasing capability: "Unleash the full power of adaptive learning"
- **Ignite** — when sparking energy: "Ignite curiosity at scale"
- **Activate** — when enabling action: "Activate personalized pathways"
- **Amplify** — when increasing impact: "Amplify educator reach"

Choose the verb that matches the energy of the sentence. Don't rotate them mechanically.

## Brand Lexicon

A2R builds a proprietary vocabulary — coined terms that become associated with the brand. These terms create recognition and differentiate A2R's language from generic industry speak.

### Active Terms

| Term | Spanish | Meaning | Use In |
|------|---------|---------|--------|
| **Cognitive Democracy** | **Democracia Cognitiva** | The principle that every learner deserves an adaptive, personalized learning path regardless of background | Website, keynotes, thought leadership, brand campaigns |

### Coining New Terms

When creating new A2R-coined terms:

1. **Evocative + Concrete**: The term should spark imagination ("Cognitive") while being grounded in education reality ("Democracy")
2. **Self-explanatory on first read**: The reader should grasp the concept even without a definition
3. **Not jargon**: It should feel like a fresh idea, not an acronym or industry buzzword
4. **Consistent use**: Once coined, use it consistently across all content — don't alternate with paraphrases
5. **Earn it**: Don't overload copy with coined terms. One per major piece is usually enough.

## The ASAR Rule

ASAR and any internal A2R mythology, codenames, or team-specific terminology are **purely internal**. They must never appear in any customer-facing material — websites, proposals, social media, emails, marketing collateral, press releases, or partner communications.

If asked to include ASAR or internal mythology references in external copy, decline and explain that this content is reserved for internal use.

## No-Emoji Rule

**Severity: HIGH** — This is a hard rule, not a style preference.

No emojis in any A2R communication — websites, proposals, social media, emails, blog posts, internal docs shared externally, or any customer-facing material.

Emojis undermine the authority and precision of A2R's voice. They signal casualness that conflicts with the expert-rebel tone. A2R conveys energy through:

- **Strong word choice**: verbs that carry their own energy ("ignite", "unlock", "shatter")
- **Sentence rhythm**: short-medium-short cadence creates momentum without visual crutches
- **Punctuation**: em dashes, colons, and periods create emphasis naturally

If a draft contains emojis, remove them and strengthen the surrounding copy to carry the same energy through words alone.

## Formatting and Structure

### Customer-Facing Copy
- **Flowing prose**: Paragraphs over bullet lists
- **Minimal formatting tricks**: No excessive bolding, no ALL CAPS for emphasis (except visual design overlines/buttons per the design system), no exclamation marks for energy
- **Em dashes (—) with restraint**: AI-generated text tends to overuse em dashes. In A2R copy, prefer **parentheses** for asides, clarifications, and secondary information. Reserve the em dash for moments of genuine dramatic pause or contrast. If a sentence has more than one em dash pair, rewrite it.
  - Parentheses: "Our platform (built over 15 years of R&D) adapts to each learner."
  - Em dash (acceptable): "We don't automate education — we reinvent it."
  - Overuse (avoid): "Our platform — built over 15 years — adapts to each learner — in real time."
- **Short paragraphs**: 2-4 sentences per paragraph
- **White space**: Let the text breathe; prefer shorter blocks with generous spacing

### Internal / Technical Content
- Bullet lists, numbered steps, and structured formatting are all appropriate
- Technical precision takes priority over prose style
- The personality pillars still apply but with lighter touch

### Numbers and Evidence
- **Specific over vague**: "40% improvement in retention" not "significant improvement"
- **Context over raw numbers**: "From 60% to 84% completion rates" tells a better story than "84% completion"
- **Verifiable claims**: Every stat in A2R copy should trace to a real source

## Punctuation Rules

### Em Dashes

Em dashes (—) are a legitimate A2R punctuation tool for creating emphasis, asides, and rhythm breaks. However:

- **Maximum one em dash pair per paragraph**: Overuse dilutes impact and creates choppy reading
- **Never stack**: Two em-dash interruptions in the same sentence is always too many
- **Prefer for emphasis, not structure**: Use em dashes to create a dramatic pause or aside — not as a substitute for commas or parentheses in routine clauses
- **Both sides**: In English, em dashes have no spaces around them ("word—word"). In Spanish, em dashes have spaces (" — ")

### Trailing Periods

- No period at the end of headlines, hero messages, or single-sentence titles
- Use a period only when the text contains more than one sentence
- This applies to both English and Spanish

### Exclamation Marks

- Never use exclamation marks for energy in customer-facing copy
- The words should carry the energy, not the punctuation
- Exception: social media posts where the tone is genuinely playful (use sparingly)
